What is off-policyness?

In production RL training, the policy used to generate rollouts is rarely identical to the policy being updated. Mismatches arise from mixed-precision inference (e.g., BF16 training with FP8 rollout generation), non-standard sampling temperatures, asynchronous pipeline depth, and other practical deployment constraints. These differences shift the per-token importance ratios away from one, making the on-policy assumption underlying standard clipping-based objectives increasingly fragile. The experiments below stress-test this directly: each one introduces a controlled off-policy perturbation and measures how training stability holds up. P3O responds through its adaptive effective sample size (ESS) regularizer, which tightens the trust region when the batch diverges from the current policy — without any retuning.

Temperature of Rollouts — Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507

Temperature 0.6

Temperature 1.2

Sampling rollouts at T ≠ 1.0 uniformly shifts per-token log-probabilities, pushing importance ratios off one across the entire batch. GRPO's fixed clip range cannot compensate; performance degrades at both T = 0.6 and T = 1.2. P3O's ESS-driven trust level adapts to the induced ratio shift and maintains stable training without retuning.

BF16 Train + FP8 Rollout — Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507

Mixed-precision pipelines that generate rollouts in FP8 and train in BF16 create systematic log-probability mismatches, particularly at long rollout lengths, a phenomenon recently documented in Jet-RL (Xi et al., 2026). GRPO collapses once the mismatch accumulates; P3O remains stable under the same conditions, enabling faster FP8 rollout generation without sacrificing training quality.

Experiments run on Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 with the DeepScaleR Preview dataset on 8 × H100 GPUs. For the full benchmark evaluation under FP8 and clip sensitivity sweeps, see the Episode Two blog post and paper.
